Gamete Anatomy — Sperm & Egg

The male and female gametes are the most structurally distinct cell types in biology — each perfectly adapted for its single purpose.

The Sperm Cell

  • Acrosome: Cap-like vesicle packed with hydrolytic enzymes (acrosin, hyaluronidase). Releases contents during the acrosomal reaction to penetrate the egg's zona pellucida.
  • Nucleus: Highly condensed; contains 23 chromosomes (haploid). DNA wrapped around protamines instead of histones — maximises compaction.
  • Midpiece: Packed with mitochondria arranged in a helix — the power plant that drives flagellar beating.
  • Flagellum: A single long tail (9+2 axoneme structure). Beats 10–20 times per second to propel the sperm at ~3 mm/min.

The Human Egg (Oocyte)

  • Corona radiata: The outermost layer of follicle cells (granulosa cells) surrounding the zona pellucida. These cells nourished the developing oocyte via gap junctions. Sperm must penetrate this layer first using acrosomal enzymes (hyaluronidase). The corona disperses after fertilization.
  • Zona pellucida (ZP1/ZP2/ZP3): A thick (~13 µm), acellular glycoprotein coat. ZP3 is the primary sperm receptor — species-specific binding triggers the acrosomal reaction. ZP2 binds acrosome-reacted sperm. After fertilization the zona hardens (slow block to polyspermy), preventing additional sperm entry.
  • Plasma membrane: The true cell boundary. On sperm-egg fusion, Na⁺ influx depolarizes the membrane within seconds — the fast block to polyspermy — immediately raising the electrical barrier to additional sperm.
  • Cytoplasm: Contains maternal mRNAs and cytoplasmic determinants distributed asymmetrically. The animal pole has the nucleus; the vegetal pole has a slight yolk enrichment. These gradients establish developmental polarity before first cleavage.
  • Yolk granules: Sparse lipid/protein nutrient stores. Mammalian eggs are isolecithal (even, minimal yolk) — nutrients are supplied by the placenta rather than stored in the egg, unlike amphibian or avian eggs.
  • Polar body: A small, non-functional cell expelled during meiosis I (first polar body) when the oocyte divides asymmetrically. It presses against the zona pellucida at the animal pole and marks polarity. A second polar body is expelled at meiosis II, after fertilization.
Cytoplasmic Determinant Maternal mRNAs and proteins that are asymmetrically localised in the egg cytoplasm. Inherited by specific daughter cells during cleavage and specify their developmental fate. Example: bicoid mRNA at the anterior pole.
Germ Layer One of the three primary tissue layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) established during gastrulation. Every adult tissue traces its origin to one of these three layers.
Somite Paired, segmented blocks of paraxial mesoderm that form sequentially along both sides of the notochord during organogenesis. Give rise to vertebrae, ribs, skeletal muscle, and dermis of the back.

Fertilization — Step by Step

Fertilization in sea urchins is the best-studied model. It involves two key reactions: the acrosomal reaction (sperm's response to the egg's jelly coat) and the cortical reaction (the egg's defence against additional sperm).

Step 1

Sperm swims toward the egg, guided by chemotactic signals (resact in sea urchins) released by the egg's jelly coat. The corona radiata is the outermost barrier. Sperm is still intact — acrosome closed, fully functional.

Step 2 — Acrosomal Reaction

Contact with ZP3 triggers exocytosis of the acrosomal vesicle. Hydrolytic enzymes (hyaluronidase, acrosin) digest through the zona pellucida. The acrosomal inner membrane, now exposed, displays Bindin — the species-specific adhesion protein.

Step 3 — Bindin & Sperm-Egg Fusion

Bindin (on the acrosomal inner membrane) binds its complementary receptor on the egg (EBR1 in sea urchin; ZP3 in mammals). This interaction is species-specific. Binding triggers membrane fusion; the sperm nucleus enters. Simultaneously, Na⁺ influx depolarises the egg plasma membrane → fast block to polyspermy (seconds).

Step 4 — Cortical Reaction (Slow Block)

Sperm entry triggers a calcium wave (Ca²⁺) that sweeps across the egg. Ca²⁺ causes cortical granules to fuse with the plasma membrane and release enzymes into the perivitelline space. These enzymes cross-link zona proteins, hardening it into the fertilisation envelope — the slow block to polyspermy, complete in ~60 seconds.

Step 5 — Zygote

The sperm pronucleus (haploid, ♂) and egg pronucleus (haploid, ♀) fuse, restoring the diploid chromosome number (2n = 46 in humans). Development is activated. The first mitotic division (cleavage) follows within hours. The fertilization envelope protects the zygote as cleavage begins.

Cleavage — Dividing Without Growing

Cleavage is a series of rapid mitotic divisions with no growth between divisions. Each daughter cell (blastomere) receives a different complement of cytoplasmic determinants, establishing the first cell fate distinctions.

Fig 2.1 · Cleavage Stages
Cleavage stages — rapid mitosis without growth. Cytoplasmic determinants are progressively partitioned into smaller, fate-determined blastomeres.
Cytoplasmic Determinants in Cleavage — Frog Example

In Xenopus (frog) eggs, VegT mRNA is localised to the vegetal pole. Cells inheriting VegT become endoderm/mesoderm. β-catenin (Wnt pathway) concentrates in dorsal vegetal cells → specifies the Spemann organiser. Animal pole cells receive neither → default ectoderm fate. The first cleavage furrow bisects both the animal and vegetal poles, so each 2-cell blastomere gets some of each → both are capable of normal development (regulatory development).

Gastrulation — Three Germ Layers Form

Gastrulation transforms the blastula into a three-layered embryo (the gastrula). Cells undergo dramatic coordinated movements — invagination, involution, convergent extension — that move presumptive endoderm and mesoderm to the interior while ectoderm covers the outside.

Fig 2.2 · Gastrulation (Sea Urchin)
Gastrulation — the blastula reorganises to form three distinct germ layers. The blastopore is the site of invagination; in deuterostomes (including vertebrates) it becomes the anus.
Fig 2.2b · Vertebrate Gastrulation — Amniote Primitive Streak (Dorsal View)
Vertebrate (amniote) gastrulation viewed from the dorsal side. Epiblast cells migrate toward the primitive streak, ingress through it, and spread laterally as mesoderm and endoderm. Hensen's node (the vertebrate equivalent of the Spemann organizer) produces cells that form the notochord and secretes BMP inhibitors that pattern the dorsal axis.

The Chordamesoderm & Notochord — The Embryo's Organizer

The chordamesoderm is the region of axial mesoderm that forms during gastrulation — cells that ingress through the dorsal lip of the blastopore (or Hensen's node in amniotes) and migrate anteriorly to form the notochord. It is the most important inductive tissue in the vertebrate embryo.

Fig 2.2c · Cross-Section of Vertebrate Embryo at Neurulation Stage (~Day 18–20)
Cross-section of the vertebrate embryo during neurulation. The notochord (gold) sits at the axial midline, sending Shh upward to pattern the neural tube and broadcasting BMP inhibitors (Chordin, Noggin) laterally to allow neural induction in the overlying ectoderm.

What Chordamesoderm Does

Cells ingress through the dorsal lip of the blastopore (amphibians) or Hensen's node (amniotes) during gastrulation. They migrate anteriorly along the midline, intercalating to form the rod-shaped notochord. The whole process converts a sheet of mesoderm into the embryo's central organizer.

Notochord Functions

  • Structural scaffold defining the anterior–posterior body axis
  • Shh signaling to pattern the ventral neural tube (floor plate, motor neurons)
  • Induces sclerotome from adjacent somites → vertebrae & ribs
  • Persists as the nucleus pulposus of intervertebral discs in adults

The Spemann Organizer

Chordamesoderm secretes BMP inhibitors (Chordin, Noggin, Follistatin) that block BMP4 signaling in the overlying ectoderm. BMP4 normally drives epidermal fate; inhibiting it allows the default neural fate to be expressed. This is how ectoderm over the notochord becomes neural tube rather than skin.

Key Concept — Spemann-Mangold Experiment (1924)

Without chordamesoderm induction, the overlying ectoderm defaults to epidermis. The Spemann-Mangold experiment showed that transplanting chordamesoderm to the ventral side of a host embryo creates a second complete embryo — second neural tube, second notochord, second somite series. This proved that induction is sufficient to reorganize the entire body axis. Chordin and Noggin from the transplanted organizer respecified ventral ectoderm to neural fate by blocking its endogenous BMP signal.

From Gastrulation to Body Axis — Step by Step

Step 1 — Origin of Chordamesoderm

During gastrulation, epiblast cells migrate toward and ingress through the dorsal lip of the blastopore (in amphibians) or Hensen's node (in amniotes). The cells that ingress at the very midline — not spreading laterally as mesoderm — are the chordamesoderm. They are distinct from the lateral plate mesoderm and paraxial mesoderm (future somites) that ingress at flanking positions.

In amphibians, the dorsal lip is the most powerful inductive region in the embryo. Spemann called it the "organizer" because transplanting it produces a second complete body axis.

Step 2 — Anterior Migration and Notochord Formation

Chordamesoderm cells migrate anteriorly along the embryonic midline, intercalating (merging side-by-side) to form a compact, rod-shaped structure — the notochord. The process of convergent extension (cells narrow mediolaterally and elongate) drives the notochord's elongation along the A–P axis.

The notochord is not a hollow tube — it is a solid rod of tightly packed cells surrounded by a fibrous sheath. It runs from the base of the skull (odontoid region) to the tail bud.

Step 3 — Neural Induction (BMP Inhibition)

The notochord secretes BMP inhibitors — primarily Chordin, Noggin, and Follistatin. These proteins bind and neutralize BMP4 in the extracellular space directly above the notochord. BMP4 normally drives overlying ectoderm toward epidermal (skin) fate. When BMP4 is inhibited, ectoderm follows its default neural fate, thickening into the neural plate.

Result: ectoderm directly above the notochord becomes neural tissue; ectoderm outside the BMP-inhibited zone remains epidermis. This inductive event is the first step of neurulation.

Step 4 — Ventral–Dorsal Patterning via Shh

The notochord secretes Sonic Hedgehog (Shh) upward into the overlying neural tube and sideways into adjacent somites. Shh establishes a ventral-to-dorsal concentration gradient with two major effects:

  • Neural tube patterning: High Shh → floor plate (ventral-most cells); moderate Shh → motor neuron progenitors (MN, V3 interneurons). This gradient divides the neural tube into distinct progenitor domains along the D–V axis.
  • Sclerotome induction: Shh from the notochord directs the ventromedial compartment of each somite to become sclerotome — cells that migrate around the notochord and neural tube to form vertebrae and ribs.
Step 5 — Fate of the Notochord

The notochord is a transient structure in vertebrates. As the vertebral column develops, sclerotome cells condense around the notochord, forming the cartilaginous primordia of the vertebrae. The notochord is gradually compressed and displaced. In the adult:

  • Most of the notochord is resorbed or replaced by bone.
  • The portion caught between adjacent vertebral bodies persists as the nucleus pulposus — the soft, gel-like center of each intervertebral disc that absorbs compressive load.

In invertebrate chordates (amphioxus, tunicates), the notochord persists throughout life as the primary structural support — there is no vertebral column.

Common exam trap: The notochord does not become the vertebral column — the vertebral column forms from sclerotome cells that migrate around the notochord and replace it. The notochord contributes only the nucleus pulposus in adults.

What Each Germ Layer Becomes

Ectoderm

"Outside" — covers the body and forms the nervous system.

  • Epidermis, hair, nails, skin glands
  • Brain, spinal cord (CNS)
  • Eyes (lens, retina), inner ear
  • Pituitary gland (anterior lobe from oral ectoderm)
  • Neural crest cells — a "4th germ layer"

Mesoderm

"Middle" — forms the structural and supportive tissues.

  • Skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle
  • Bones and cartilage
  • Kidneys and gonads
  • Heart, blood vessels, blood cells
  • Connective tissue, dermis, spleen
  • Somites → vertebrae, ribs, back muscle

Endoderm

"Inside" — lines internal cavities and organs.

  • Entire gut epithelium (mouth to anus)
  • Liver and gallbladder
  • Pancreas
  • Lungs (respiratory epithelium)
  • Thyroid, parathyroid
  • Urinary bladder lining
Exam Insight: The mucosa of the gut is endoderm; the muscles of the gut wall are mesoderm; the neurons of the enteric nervous system come from neural crest cells (derived from ectoderm). One organ, three germ layers.

Nervous System Development — Neurulation

The nervous system develops from dorsal ectoderm, induced by the underlying chordamesoderm (notochord precursor) via inductive signalling. This is one of the clearest examples of embryonic induction.

Fig 2.3 · Neurulation — 4 Stages
Neurulation — chordamesoderm induces neural plate formation; folds elevate and fuse to form the neural tube; neural crest cells emigrate to diverse destinations
Failure of neural tube closure causes neural tube defects. Anencephaly (failure anteriorly → no brain) and spina bifida (failure posteriorly → exposed spinal cord). Folic acid supplementation reduces risk because folate is required for cell division during tube closure.
Fig 2.3b · Signaling in the Neural Tube — Dorsal–Ventral Patterning
Shh from the notochord diffuses upward through the floor plate into the neural tube, creating a ventral-to-dorsal concentration gradient. BMP4 and Wnt from the dorsal ectoderm create the opposing gradient. Each progenitor domain responds to the local signal ratio to activate specific transcription factors and produce a distinct neuron type.

Somite Formation

Somites are segmented blocks of paraxial mesoderm that bud off sequentially along both sides of the notochord, forming in an anterior-to-posterior wave. They are the structural basis of the vertebral column's segmentation.

Fig 2.4 · Somite Formation & Differentiation
Somites bud off sequentially from paraxial mesoderm — always anterior to posterior — forming in bilateral pairs flanking the notochord
Fig 2.4b · Signals That Specify Somite Compartments
Somite compartments are specified by competing signals: Shh from the notochord drives sclerotome (ventromedial) fate; Wnt from the neural tube drives dermomyotome (dorsolateral) fate; BMP from the lateral plate marks the boundary of the somite. The interplay of these gradients defines all three structural derivatives.
Fig 2.4c · The Segmentation Clock & FGF/RA Wavefront
The segmentation clock (Notch/Wnt/FGF oscillations in the PSM) interacts with the FGF/RA counter-gradients to set the determination front. Each time the clock "ticks" at the determination front, a new somite boundary is cut — anterior to posterior. Clock period = segment size.

Sclerotome → Skeleton

Ventromedial cells of the somite migrate toward the notochord and form vertebrae and ribs. Notochord signals (Shh) drive sclerotome fate.

Myotome → Muscle

Dorsomedial cells form the deep back muscles (epaxial). Lateral myotome cells migrate into limb buds to form limb muscles.

Dermatome → Dermis

Dorsolateral cells spread under the epidermis to form the dermis of the back. Each dermatome corresponds to a specific skin segment, giving rise to sensory nerve distributions (dermatomes in anatomy).

Key Numbers: Segmentation Clock

Mouse: one new somite pair every ~90 min. Human: every ~6 h. Snakes have dramatically more somites (200–400+) than mice (~65) — evolutionary changes in clock period and wavefront speed are a primary mechanism for body-plan diversification across vertebrates. If the clock is genetically disrupted (e.g., lunatic fringe mutants in mice), somites fuse and vertebrae are malformed, causing congenital scoliosis-like defects.

Level 3 · Apply & Analyse

Predicting Disruption Outcomes

The real test of understanding is predicting what goes wrong when the process is disrupted. For each scenario below, identify the exact step affected and what the downstream consequence would be.

If…
The bindin receptor (EBR1) on the sea urchin egg is mutated and cannot bind Bindin
Then…
Sperm cannot fuse with the egg. The acrosomal reaction still occurs (triggered by the jelly coat), but without Bindin-receptor binding, membrane fusion fails. No fertilization occurs. Also note: because Bindin-EBR1 interactions are species-specific, this would also prevent cross-species fertilization.
If…
The cortical reaction is blocked (e.g., by chelating all intracellular Ca²⁺)
Then…
The fast block (membrane depolarisation) may still work briefly, but the cortical granules never fuse with the plasma membrane. The fertilisation envelope does not form. Multiple sperm can penetrate (polyspermy). Polyspermic zygotes have too many chromosomes (triploid, tetraploid…) and cannot divide normally → embryo is lethal.
If…
Chordamesoderm tissue is transplanted to the ventral side of a host embryo (Spemann organiser experiment)
Then…
A second complete axis forms on the ventral side — a second neural tube, second notochord, second set of somites. This is the Spemann–Mangold organiser experiment. The host cells respond to inductive signals (BMP inhibitors: chordin, noggin, follistatin) from the transplanted tissue. Proves that induction is real and sufficient to reorganise axis identity.
If…
Neural tube closure fails in the posterior region
Then…
Spina bifida results — the spinal cord and/or meninges protrude through the unfused vertebral arches. Neural crest migration in that region may also be disrupted, affecting the PNS and dermis. Folic acid deficiency is the primary environmental risk factor, as folate is essential for the rapid cell division required during tube closure.
If…
The segmentation clock period is doubled (each cycle takes twice as long)
Then…
Somites form half as frequently relative to the embryo's elongation. Because the wavefront still recedes at the normal rate, more paraxial mesoderm is added before each somite boundary forms. Result: fewer, larger somites → fewer vertebrae and ribs in the adult. This is exactly the mechanism by which snake body plans may have evolved — regulatory changes in clock/wavefront coupling produce radically different vertebral counts.
If…
Neural crest cells fail to emigrate from the neural tube
Then…
A broad range of defects collectively called neurocristopathies. Depending on which neural crest populations are affected: no pigment cells → albinism; no craniofacial cartilage → severe facial malformations; no enteric neurons → Hirschsprung disease (no gut motility); no adrenal medulla → loss of adrenaline production; defects in the peripheral and cardiac outflow tract.

Practice Questions

Why is the cortical reaction essential? What would happen with and without it? ▾
The cortical reaction forms the fertilisation envelope — a hardened zona that physically prevents additional sperm from penetrating (slow block to polyspermy). Without it, multiple sperm could fertilise the same egg. Polyspermy results in an abnormal chromosome number (triploid, tetraploid) because each sperm adds a full haploid set. Triploid embryos cannot undergo normal meiosis later and die. The fast block (membrane depolarisation) provides immediate, temporary protection within seconds; the cortical reaction provides lasting, structural protection over 1–2 minutes.
How do cytoplasmic determinants create cell diversity during cleavage if all cells have the same DNA? ▾
Cytoplasmic determinants (maternal mRNAs and proteins) are asymmetrically distributed in the egg. When the egg divides, the cleavage planes bisect the cytoplasm unevenly — some blastomeres inherit specific determinants and others do not. The DNA is copied identically to every cell, but the transcription factors (encoded by those determinant mRNAs) activate different gene subsets in different cells. For example, in Drosophila, bicoid mRNA is at the anterior → anterior blastomeres get Bicoid protein → activate anterior gap genes. Posterior blastomeres get Nanos → different gene expression programme.
A student says that the notochord eventually becomes the vertebral column. Is this correct? Explain. ▾
Partially correct but misleading. The notochord induces the surrounding sclerotome (derived from somites) to form the vertebrae that eventually surround and mostly replace it. The notochord itself largely degenerates once the vertebrae form — only the nucleus pulposus (the gel-like centre of each intervertebral disc) is a remnant of the notochord. So: the notochord is the inducer and scaffold; the vertebrae are a mesoderm-derived structure that the notochord induces from somite cells.
