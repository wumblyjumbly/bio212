⬡ Level 1 · Remember & Identify
Core Vocabulary

The Language of Evolution

Biological Evolution
A change in the heritable characteristics of a population over successive generations — measured as a change in allele frequencies.
Population
A group of individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time, capable of interbreeding and sharing a common gene pool.
Gene
A segment of DNA that encodes a functional product (typically a protein or RNA). The unit of heredity.
Allele
One of two or more alternative versions of a gene. Different alleles arise by mutation and may produce different phenotypes.
Mutation
A heritable change in the nucleotide sequence of DNA. The ultimate source of all new alleles — the raw material of evolution.
Allele Frequency
The proportion of a particular allele among all copies of that gene in a population. Evolution = change in allele frequency over time.
Individual vs. Population: Individual organisms do not evolve — their DNA is fixed at conception. Evolution happens to populations across generations as allele frequencies shift.
The Four Forces

Four Primary Evolutionary Processes

Every change in allele frequency in a population is driven by one (or more) of these four mechanisms. Together they determine the trajectory of a population's gene pool.

🔬

Natural Selection

Differential survival and reproduction based on heritable traits.

↓ or ↑ diversity

Effect depends on type; can increase, maintain, or decrease genetic diversity.

🎲

Genetic Drift

Random changes in allele frequency due to chance sampling events.

↓ Diversity

Strongest in small populations. Reduces variation through fixation or loss of alleles.

🌊

Gene Flow

Movement of alleles between populations through migration.

↑ Diversity

Introduces new alleles into a population; homogenises allele frequencies between populations.

⚡

Mutation

Random errors in DNA replication or repair that create new alleles.

↑ Diversity

The ultimate source of all new alleles. Rate is slow; rarely drives rapid evolution alone.

Selection Types

Four Types of Natural Selection

Directional Stabilizing Balancing Disruptive
Mnemonic D · S · B · D "Driving Shift, Balanced Divide" — push one extreme, hold the middle, hold both, split in two.
⬡ Level 2 · Understand & Explain
The Null Hypothesis

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Before we can measure evolution, we need a baseline — a mathematical model of what a population looks like when evolution is not occurring. That baseline is Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.

p + q = 1
p² + 2pq + q² = 1
p = freq(dominant allele) · q = freq(recessive allele)
p² = freq(AA) · 2pq = freq(Aa) · q² = freq(aa)
Five H-W assumptions (violating any = evolution is occurring):
  1. No mutation
  2. No gene flow (closed population)
  3. Random mating (panmixia)
  4. No genetic drift (infinitely large population)
  5. No natural selection (all genotypes equally fit)

Process 01

Natural Selection

Mechanism: Individuals with heritable traits that improve survival or reproduction pass those traits on at higher rates. Over generations, advantageous allele frequencies increase in the population.

Darwin's Three Requirements

🔁
Variation
Heritable differences exist within the population
⚖️
Differential Fitness
Some variants survive/reproduce better
🧬
Heritability
Advantageous traits are passed to offspring

Directional Selection

Favours one extreme phenotype. The population mean shifts in one direction over generations; genetic diversity decreases as disfavoured alleles are removed.

Examples: Antibiotic resistance — bacteria with resistance alleles survive, sensitive ones die. Beak depth in Galápagos finches during drought when only large hard seeds remain.

Stabilizing Selection

Favours the intermediate phenotype and selects against both extremes. The distribution becomes taller and narrower. Diversity decreases.

Example: Human birth weight — very low and very high birth weights both have reduced survival; intermediate weights are favoured. Human gestational length shows a similar pattern.

Balancing Selection

Maintains multiple alleles in a population simultaneously. Diversity is maintained. Includes heterozygote advantage (heterosis) and negative frequency-dependent selection.

Example: Sickle-cell anaemia in malaria-endemic regions — HbA/HbS heterozygotes are more resistant to malaria than either homozygote, so both HbA and HbS alleles persist in the population at stable frequencies.

Disruptive Selection

Favours both extremes over the intermediate phenotype. The population splits into two phenotypic clusters. Diversity increases and can be a driver of speciation.

Example: African seedcracker finches — large beaks crack hard seeds efficiently, small beaks crack soft seeds efficiently; intermediate beak size is poor at both, so both extremes are favoured over the middle.

Process 02

Genetic Drift

Mechanism: In any finite population, allele frequencies change by random chance from generation to generation — some individuals leave more offspring simply because of luck, not because of fitness advantages.

Genetic drift is strongest in small populations and can cause alleles to be lost or fixed entirely regardless of their adaptive value. It always reduces genetic diversity.

Bottleneck Effect

Example: Cheetahs — a near-extinction event left so little genetic diversity that skin grafts are rarely rejected between individuals.

Founder Effect

Example: Amish communities — Ellis van Creveld syndrome (extra fingers, short limbs) is far more common due to one founding couple carrying the allele.

Process 03

Gene Flow

Mechanism: The movement of alleles between populations via the migration of individuals (or gametes). Gene flow introduces new alleles and tends to make populations more genetically similar.

Effect on Genetic Diversity

  • Receiving population: diversity ↑ (new alleles arrive)
  • Between populations: allele frequencies homogenise
  • Net effect: opposes divergence and speciation

Examples

  • Pollen dispersal by wind between plant populations
  • Birds migrating and breeding in new territories
  • Human migration and interbreeding between groups
Process 04

Mutation

Mechanism: Random changes in DNA sequence — substitutions, insertions, deletions — create new alleles. Mutation is the only process that introduces entirely new genetic information into a population.

Key Points

  • Mutation rate: ~10⁻⁸ per base pair per generation in humans
  • Most mutations are neutral (synonymous, non-coding)
  • Rarely beneficial; commonly deleterious
  • Increases genetic diversity
  • Provides raw material for all other evolutionary processes

Types of Mutation

  • Point mutation: single base substitution
  • Insertion/Deletion: frameshift; often severe
  • Gene duplication: extra copy free to diverge
  • Chromosomal rearrangement: inversions, translocations

Scenario Practice

Identify the Evolutionary Process

Read each scenario and identify which of the four evolutionary processes is at work. Click "Reveal" to check your answer.

Scenario 01

A volcanic eruption wipes out 97% of a beetle population. The 3% that survive happen to carry a rare green colouration allele. Over the next 20 generations, nearly all beetles are green — not because green beetles are better camouflaged, but simply because that was what the survivors happened to carry.

Genetic Drift — Bottleneck Effect. The change was due to random survival, not selection pressure on the colour trait. The population size crash caused chance fixation of the green allele.
Scenario 02

In a population of moths exposed to pesticides, moths with a resistance allele survive and reproduce. Over ten generations, the resistance allele goes from 2% to 89% frequency. Susceptible moths are nearly gone.

Natural Selection — Directional. The resistance allele confers fitness advantage; the environment consistently favours one extreme phenotype, shifting allele frequency directionally.
Scenario 03

A small group of ten rabbits crosses a newly formed land bridge from the mainland to an island. The island population grows to thousands, but has much lower genetic diversity than the mainland population and a high frequency of a previously rare ear-shape allele that the founders happened to carry.

Genetic Drift — Founder Effect. The small founding group did not represent the full allele diversity of the mainland. Random allele frequencies in the founders became the new baseline.
Scenario 04

A previously rare blood type allele suddenly appears in a prairie dog population at 12% frequency. Researchers confirm no prairie dogs died or were born unusually — the frequency changed because several prairie dogs emigrated from a neighbouring colony where that allele was common.

Gene Flow. Migration of individuals carrying the allele from another population introduced it into the receiving population — a textbook case of gene flow.
Scenario 05

In a sequencing study, scientists discover a brand-new DNA variant in a single individual of a fish species that has never been seen in any other individual of that species — or in any other species. The variant alters an enzyme's active site.

Mutation. A de novo change in DNA sequence created a new allele that did not previously exist in the population. This is mutation — the only process that generates genuinely new genetic information.
Scenario 06

In a population of mice living in a snowy environment, both very dark and very light mice are frequently caught by predators. Mice with intermediate grey colouration survive and reproduce most successfully. Over many generations, the population converges on intermediate colouration.

Natural Selection — Stabilizing. Both extreme phenotypes are selected against; intermediate phenotype has highest fitness. The distribution narrows around the mean.
Scenario 07

In a population of oysters living in a tidal zone, both very large and very small oysters survive well — large ones resist physical disturbance while small ones fit in crevices. Intermediate-sized oysters are most vulnerable to predation. Over generations, the size distribution becomes bimodal.

Natural Selection — Disruptive. Both extremes are favoured over the intermediate. The distribution splits into two peaks, potentially leading to ecological specialisation or even speciation.
Scenario 08

The HbS allele (sickle-cell) never disappears from a West African population despite causing severe disease in homozygotes. Researchers find that HbA/HbS heterozygotes have better survival rates in malaria-endemic regions than HbA/HbA homozygotes. Both alleles persist at stable frequencies for centuries.

Natural Selection — Balancing (heterozygote advantage). The heterozygote is more fit than either homozygote, so selection maintains both alleles in the population indefinitely. This is a classic case of balancing selection.
Practice Questions

Test Your Understanding

Q1: A population satisfies all five H-W assumptions. You measure allele frequencies and find that p = 0.6. What percentage of the population would you expect to be heterozygous carriers?
2pq = 2 × 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.48 = 48%. The population is in H-W equilibrium, so genotype frequencies follow p² + 2pq + q² = 1.
Q2: Why does genetic drift have a stronger effect in small populations than large ones?
In a small population, sampling error is proportionally larger. If only 10 individuals reproduce, a random absence of one allele wipes it out entirely. In a population of 10,000, random absences average out and allele frequencies remain stable. The effect is inversely proportional to population size (∝ 1/2N).
Q3: Disruptive selection is sometimes called a driver of speciation. Why?
Disruptive selection favours two extreme phenotypes simultaneously. This can lead to bimodal phenotype distributions and, if assortative mating follows (extreme morphs preferentially mate with like phenotypes), to reproductive isolation. Over time, the two morphs may diverge enough to become separate species — a process called sympatric speciation.
Q4: How does gene flow affect speciation?
Gene flow counteracts speciation by homogenising allele frequencies between populations, preventing the genetic divergence needed to form separate species. Geographic isolation (allopatry) prevents gene flow, which is why it is a major driver of speciation. High gene flow = populations remain genetically similar = no speciation.
