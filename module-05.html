⬡ Level 1 · Remember & Identify
Animal Kingdom

What Distinguishes Animals?

Kingdom Animalia is defined by a set of shared derived characters (synapomorphies) not found together in any other kingdom.

🔬
Eukaryotic & Multicellular

All animals are multicellular eukaryotes. No animal is unicellular (unlike protists). Cell differentiation into tissues is a defining feature of most animals.

🍖
Heterotrophic

Animals cannot synthesise organic molecules from inorganic sources (no photosynthesis). All animals ingest other organisms or their products. No cell walls (unlike plants and fungi).

🏃
Motility

Most animals are motile at some stage of their life cycle — typically by muscle contraction. Even sessile adults (sponges, barnacles) often have motile larval stages.

🧬
Sexual Reproduction & Diploid Dominance

Animals are typically diploid for most of their life cycle. Gametes are the only haploid stage. Most reproduce sexually; the egg is typically larger than the sperm.

🥚
Embryonic Development

All animals develop through embryonic stages: fertilisation → cleavage → blastula → gastrula. Embryonic development is a unique shared feature of the animal kingdom.

🕸
Collagen & Extracellular Matrix

Animals produce collagen — a structural protein unique to animals — and have an extracellular matrix (ECM) holding cells together. No cell walls of cellulose or chitin surround animal cells.

Body Plan

What is a Body Plan?

◈ Definition

Body plan: The set of morphological and developmental features shared by members of a phylum — the overall organisation of an animal's body, including the arrangement of tissues, organ systems, and symmetry. Body plans are largely established during early embryonic development (gastrulation and beyond) and are phylogenetically conserved within phyla.

Four primary aspects of a body plan are used to classify animals and reflect key evolutionary innovations:

Primary Aspect 01

Number of Germ Layers

Diploblastic: 2 layers — ectoderm + endoderm only. No true mesoderm.

Triploblastic: 3 layers — ecto + meso + endo. Mesoderm enables muscles, organs, coelom.

Cnidaria = diploblastic All Bilateria = triploblastic
Primary Aspect 02

Body Symmetry

Asymmetric: No plane of symmetry (sponges).

Radial: Multiple planes through central axis. Sessile/slow-moving.

Bilateral: One plane of symmetry (dorsal/ventral, anterior/posterior). Associated with active movement and cephalization.

Sponges = asymmetric Cnidaria = radial Bilateria = bilateral
Primary Aspect 03

Body Cavity (Coelom)

Acoelomate: No body cavity. Mesoderm fills space between gut and body wall (e.g., flatworms).

Pseudocoelomate: Cavity present but NOT fully lined by mesoderm (e.g., nematodes).

Eucoelomate: True coelom fully lined by mesoderm (peritoneum). Provides space for organs (e.g., annelids, molluscs, arthropods, chordates).

Primary Aspect 04

Early Development Pattern

Protostomes: Spiral + determinate cleavage; blastopore → mouth; schizocoely (coelom from mesoderm splits).

Deuterostomes: Radial + indeterminate cleavage; blastopore → anus; enterocoely (coelom from gut pockets).

Insects, worms, molluscs = protostome Chordates, echinoderms = deuterostome
Protostomia

Two Major Lineages of Protostomes

Clade 1

Lophotrochozoa

Named for the lophophore (a ciliated feeding structure) and/or the trochophore larva (a free-swimming ciliated larval form). Members share one or both of these features.

Clade 2

Ecdysozoa

Named for ecdysis — the periodic moulting of a hard external cuticle. All ecdysozoans grow by shedding their cuticle rather than by continuously expanding it.

Body Plan Details

Secondary Aspects of Body Plans

Cephalization
Concentration of sensory organs and the nervous system at the anterior end, forming a head. Associated with bilateral symmetry and active directional movement. Allows rapid processing of information encountered at the leading end of the body. Varies from minimal (flatworms — anterior ganglia only) to extreme (cephalopods — sophisticated brain, camera eyes).
Segmentation (Metamerism)
Serial repetition of body units (segments or metameres) along the A-P axis, each containing copies of organ systems. True metamerism (same set of organs in each segment) in Annelida. Tagmatization in Arthropoda = segments fused into specialised functional groups (tagmata): head, thorax, abdomen. Enables regional specialisation while retaining a modular developmental programme.
Nervous System Organisation
Nerve net: no centralisation, diffuse (Cnidaria). Ladder-type: paired ventral nerve cords with ganglia in each segment (Platyhelminthes, Annelida). Fused ganglia: brain + ventral nerve cord in Arthropoda. Highly centralised: Cephalopods — the most complex invertebrate nervous system. Complexity correlates with ecological niche and behavioural repertoire.
Limbs & Appendages
Arthropod appendages are jointed (articulated by muscles across a rigid exoskeleton joint), enabling precise fine motor control. Biramous appendages (two branches) in ancestral crustaceans. Uniramous (one branch) in insects, myriapods, arachnids. Molluscs have a muscular foot (not a jointed limb). Annelids may have parapodia (unjointed fleshy lobes). Vertebrate limbs are endoskeletal.
Fig 5.2 · Body Cavity Types — Cross-Section
Body cavity types. Acoelomates (Platyhelminthes) have mesoderm filling the space between ectoderm and gut with no body cavity. Pseudocoelomates (Nematoda) have a body cavity not fully lined by mesoderm. Eucoelomates have a true coelom fully lined by mesoderm (peritoneum) on both sides.

Phylum

Mollusca (second most diverse animal phylum)

~100,000 living species: snails, clams, octopus, squid, chitons, nudibranchs

Shared molluscan features: muscular foot (locomotion), mantle (dorsal tissue fold that secretes shell), mantle cavity (contains gills/ctenidia for gas exchange), radula (toothed feeding ribbon, absent in bivalves), visceral mass (digestive/reproductive organs), trochophore larva → veliger larva.

Gastropoda — snails & slugs

Largest mollusk class (~70,000 sp.). Defined by torsion — a 180° rotation of the visceral mass relative to the head and foot during larval development, bringing the mantle cavity (and anus) over the head. Consequences: U-shaped gut, potential fouling of the mantle cavity (resolved by evolution of an asymmetric body). Most have a single coiled shell (reduced or absent in slugs and sea slugs). Radula present; herbivores, predators, or parasites. Terrestrial species (snails, slugs) breathe via a vascularised mantle cavity (lung); aquatic species use ctenidia.

TorsionSingle coiled shellRadula presentTerrestrial + aquatic

Polyplacophora — chitons

~1,000 species of marine, rock-clinging molluscs. The most morphologically "primitive" class. Defined by 8 overlapping dorsal shell plates (valves) — the only molluscs with a multi-part shell. Broad muscular foot clings tenaciously to rock surfaces. Girdle (thick mantle edge) surrounds the plates. Pairs of ctenidia run in a groove around the foot. Radula present. No cephalization (head not distinct, no eyes or tentacles on head). Scrape algae and encrusting organisms from rock surfaces. No free larval stage.

8 shell platesBroad adhesive footNo cephalizationMarine only

Bivalvia — clams, mussels, oysters, scallops

~15,000 species. Defined by two hinged shells (valves) joined by a ligament and adductor muscles. No radula — unique among major mollusk classes. Most are suspension/filter feeders: enlarged ctenidia (gills) trap particles from the water current; labial palps sort particles. No distinct head. Open circulatory system. Many sessile as adults (mussels attach via byssal threads; oysters cement to substrate). Scallops can swim by clapping valves. Commercially significant: oysters, clams, mussels, geoducks, pearl oysters.

Two shells (valves)No radulaFilter feedersNo head

Cephalopoda — octopus, squid, nautilus, cuttlefish

~800 living species. The most derived molluscs — reduced or internalised shell (squid pen, cuttlebone) or absent (octopus), except Nautilus (external coiled shell). Foot modified into tentacles (arms) with suckers surrounding the head. Jet propulsion via muscular siphon. Most sophisticated invertebrate nervous system — large brain, complex learning and memory. Camera eyes that rival vertebrate eyes in resolution (convergent evolution). Closed circulatory system (unique among molluscs — high metabolic demand). Chromatophores enable rapid colour change for camouflage and communication. Ink gland for defence.

Closed circulationTentacles + siphonCamera eyesComplex brain
Ecdysozoa

Ecdysozoa — Nematoda & Arthropoda

◈ Defining Feature

Ecdysis — all ecdysozoans grow by periodically shedding (moulting) a hard external cuticle. Growth occurs in the soft interval between moults. The hormone ecdysone (a steroid) triggers moulting in arthropods. This innovation solved the growth-constraint problem of rigid external coverings and also enables metamorphosis in insects.

Phylum

Arthropoda (most diverse animal phylum)

~1.2 million described species — insects, spiders, crabs, lobsters, centipedes, mites, barnacles

Shared arthropod features: segmented body with tagmatization (segments fused into functional regions), jointed appendages (one pair per segment, at minimum), hard chitinous exoskeleton (moulted via ecdysis), open circulatory system (hemocoel — hemolymph bathes organs directly), well-developed brain (fused head ganglia) + ventral nerve cord.

Crustacea — shrimp, crabs, lobsters, barnacles, copepods

~67,000 species. Primarily aquatic (freshwater and marine); some terrestrial (wood lice, coconut crabs). Distinguishing features: two pairs of antennae (unique among arthropods — all others have at most one pair or none), biramous appendages (two-branched: exopodite + endopodite), calcified exoskeleton (calcium carbonate deposited in chitin), nauplius larva in many species. Tagmata: cephalothorax (head + thorax fused = cephalon + thorax protected by carapace) + abdomen. Gills for gas exchange. Economically and ecologically critical — copepods are the most abundant animal in the ocean.

2 pairs antennaeBiramous appendagesCalcified exoskeletonNauplius larva

Myriapoda — centipedes & millipedes

~16,000 species. All terrestrial. Defined by a body divided into head + elongated trunk with many leg-bearing segments (the name means "many-legged"). One pair of antennae. Centipedes (Chilopoda): one pair of legs per segment; predatory; first pair of legs modified into venomous forcipules for prey capture; fast-moving. Millipedes (Diplopoda): two pairs of legs per segment (two segments fused = diplosegment); detritivores (eat decomposing plant matter); slow; defensive coiling and chemical defence (cyanide compounds in some). Gas exchange via tracheae (air tubes opening at spiracles).

Many leg-bearing segmentsTerrestrial1 pair antennaeTracheae

Chelicerata — spiders, scorpions, ticks, mites, horseshoe crabs

~100,000 species. Defined by chelicerae (first pair of appendages modified as fangs or pincers for feeding/defence — not legs) and pedipalps (second pair, used for sensing or grasping). No antennae (unique among major arthropod groups). Body divided into prosoma (cephalothorax = head + thorax fused) + opisthosoma (abdomen). 4 pairs of walking legs. Arachnids (spiders, scorpions, ticks, mites) are entirely terrestrial or parasitic; horseshoe crabs (Xiphosura) are marine and ancient (little changed in 450 Ma). Spiders produce silk; chelicerae in spiders deliver venom. Gas exchange via book lungs (leaf-like respiratory structures) and/or tracheae.

Chelicerae (no mandibles)No antennae4 pairs walking legsProsoma + opisthosoma

Insecta — the most species-rich animal class (~1 million described)

Distinguishing features: body divided into 3 tagmata — head (1 pair antennae, compound eyes, mouthparts), thorax (3 pairs walking legs, typically 1–2 pairs wings), abdomen (no legs; digestive, reproductive systems). Wings are unique to insects among arthropods (and among invertebrates entirely). Gas exchange via tracheal system — tubes delivering O₂ directly to tissues. Malpighian tubules for excretion (conserve water — key to terrestrial success). Two modes of metamorphosis: incomplete (hemimetabolous) — egg → nymph → adult (grasshoppers, cockroaches); complete (holometabolous) — egg → larva → pupa → adult (beetles, butterflies, flies, bees) — enables larvae and adults to exploit different resources, reducing intraspecific competition.

3 tagmata3 pairs legsWingsTracheal systemMalpighian tubules
