Level 1 · Know

The Five Essential Developmental Processes

Every multicellular organism — from a nematode to a human — builds itself using the same five core processes. They work together, often simultaneously, and disruptions to any one cause profound developmental defects.

01

Cell Proliferation

Mitotic divisions increase cell number — from a single zygote to ~37 trillion adult cells. Proliferation must be tightly controlled: too little stunts growth; uncontrolled proliferation produces tumours.

Example → During early cleavage, a human zygote divides every 12–24 h, reaching the 16-cell morula in days — all without any increase in total cell mass.
02

Cell–Cell Interactions

Cells communicate via paracrine signals, direct contact (gap junctions, Notch), and morphogen gradients. One cell (the inducer) changes the fate of its neighbour (the responder).

Example → The notochord induces the overlying ectoderm to become neural plate (primary induction) via FGF and Wnt signals.
03

Cell Differentiation

Cells with identical DNA acquire distinct morphology, gene expression, and function. Differentiation is driven by transcription factors that activate tissue-specific gene programs while silencing others.

Example → MyoD expression converts fibroblasts into muscle cells; Ngn2 converts fibroblasts into neurons — same DNA, radically different identity.
04

Cell Movement & Expansion

Cells migrate individually (neural crest) or as sheets (gastrulation). Tissues fold, elongate, and reshape through cytoskeletal forces and changes in cell adhesion. This sculpts organs from flat sheets of cells.

Example → Neural crest cells undergo epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition and migrate throughout the embryo, forming craniofacial bones, peripheral nerves, and melanocytes.
05

Programmed Cell Death (Apoptosis)

Specific cells are genetically programmed to die at precise times and locations — not by necrosis, but by an ordered self-destruction program (caspase cascade). Apoptosis sculpts structures by removing tissue.

Example → Fingers are separated by apoptosis of the interdigital webbing. Tadpole tails are resorbed by apoptosis during metamorphosis.

The Three Major Body Axes

Before any organ can form, the embryo must establish a coordinate system. Three perpendicular axes provide positional information for every cell. Gradients of signalling molecules (morphogens) encode this information.

Anterior-Posterior — Head ↔ Tail

Runs from head (anterior) to tail (posterior) — in an upright human, this is the top-to-bottom axis. In Drosophila, Bicoid protein diffuses from the anterior pole creating a gradient that activates head and thorax genes. Nanos anchored at the posterior represses hunchback translation posteriorly. The two opposing gradients give every nucleus a unique positional identity along this axis.

Dorsal-Ventral — Back ↔ Belly

Dorsal = back (spine, neural tube). Ventral = belly (gut, heart). In vertebrates, BMP (Bone Morphogenetic Protein) promotes ventral identity. The Spemann organizer secretes BMP inhibitors — Chordin, Noggin, Follistatin — on the dorsal side, creating an opposing gradient. Low BMP → dorsal neural tube and brain; high BMP → ventral skin and gut. In a front-view human, this axis runs front-to-back (into the page).

Left-Right Axis

Established by motile cilia at the embryonic node generating a leftward fluid current. This asymmetric flow activates Nodal signalling on the left side only, which activates Pitx2 on the left. These signals direct organ laterality: the heart loops rightward, stomach tilts left, and the liver is right-dominant. Disruption causes situs inversus — mirror-image organ placement.

Three orthogonal axes in an upright human. A-P = head to tail (vertical). D-V = back to belly (front-to-back depth in this view). L-R = left to right. Click an axis to explore how each is established by morphogen gradients.

Core Vocabulary & The Central Dogma

Transcription The synthesis of an RNA molecule from a DNA template, catalysed by RNA polymerase. The DNA sequence is "read" and an mRNA copy is produced. Occurs in the nucleus.
Translation The synthesis of a polypeptide (protein) from an mRNA template, catalysed by ribosomes in the cytoplasm. Each codon (3 nucleotides) specifies one amino acid.
Gene Expression The entire process by which information encoded in a gene is used to produce a functional product — either an RNA or a protein. A gene is "expressed" when it is being actively transcribed (and translated, if protein-coding).
Transcription Factor (TF) A protein that binds specific DNA sequences (often in gene promoters or enhancers) and activates or represses transcription of target genes. TFs are the primary mechanism by which cells regulate which genes are expressed. Example: Bicoid in Drosophila, MyoD in muscle cells.
Regulatory Cascade A series of sequential gene-regulatory events in which the protein product of one gene acts as a transcription factor controlling the next set of genes — which in turn control further downstream genes. Each tier narrows and refines spatial information.

The Central Dogma — Gene expression converts heritable DNA sequence information into functional protein products that drive cell identity
★ Key Insight

Every cell in your body contains the same ~20,000 genes. What makes a liver cell different from a neuron is not what DNA they have, but which subset of genes they express. Development is fundamentally a problem of gene regulation.

Level 2 · Understand

Differential Gene Expression — The Engine of Differentiation

If all cells share the same genome, how do they become so different? The answer is differential gene expression: the subset of genes active in any given cell determines its identity, shape, and function. Two master mechanisms create these differences:

Cytoplasmic Determinants

Maternal mRNAs and proteins are asymmetrically distributed in the egg. When the zygote divides, daughter cells inherit different amounts of these molecules, launching different gene expression programmes.

Example → bicoid mRNA is anchored at the anterior pole of the Drosophila egg. Only anterior cells get high Bicoid protein, activating anterior fate genes.

Autonomous specification — fate determined by internal contents, independent of position or neighbours.

Inductive Signalling

Cells secrete paracrine signals (ligands) that bind receptors on neighbouring cells, activating transcription factors and changing which genes are expressed. One tissue induces another.

Example → The notochord secretes Sonic Hedgehog (Shh), which diffuses into the overlying neural tube and instructs ventral neurons to become motor neurons (at high [Shh]) vs interneurons (at intermediate [Shh]).

Conditional specification — fate depends on signals from the environment and neighbours.

Bicoid — The Master Anterior Morphogen

Source

Nurse cells (15 helper cells surrounding the oocyte) synthesise bicoid mRNA during oogenesis and deposit it at the anterior pole of the developing egg. This deposition is guided by cytoskeletal anchoring.

Location

After fertilisation, bicoid mRNA is translated in place. The Bicoid protein diffuses posteriorly and is progressively degraded, creating an exponential gradient: highest anteriorly, fading to near-zero posteriorly.

Function

Bicoid is a homeodomain-containing transcription factor. At high concentration it activates hunchback (gap gene → head/thorax). It also directly represses posterior-specific genes. Concentration thresholds determine which gap genes are expressed at each position.

Bicoid knockout (bicoid⁻): The anterior end develops posterior identity instead — the larva has a mirror-image abdomen replacing its head and thorax. This proves Bicoid is necessary for anterior identity.

Ectopic Bicoid injection: Injecting bicoid mRNA into the posterior produces a second head at that end. This proves Bicoid is sufficient to specify anterior identity — concentration is everything.

Hox Genes — The Segment Identity Code

Once the regulatory cascade has divided the embryo into segments, Hox genes assign each segment its specific identity. They are homeodomain-containing transcription factors organised into clusters on the chromosome.

The Collinearity Rule

The order of Hox genes on the chromosome mirrors the order of their expression domains along the anterior-posterior axis. Genes at the 3′ end of the cluster are expressed anteriorly; genes at the 5′ end are expressed posteriorly. This spatial collinearity is conserved across all bilaterians.

Fig 1.3 · Hox Gene Collinearity (Drosophila)
Hox gene clusters — chromosomal position predicts anterior-to-posterior expression domain. This rule holds from flies to humans.
Homeotic Mutation — Antennapedia

A gain-of-function mutation in Antennapedia causes legs to grow where antennae should be. The wrong Hox gene is expressed in the wrong segment, substituting leg identity for antenna identity. Hox genes are master identity switches, not structural genes.

Why do vertebrates have 4 clusters? Two rounds of whole-genome duplication (the "2R hypothesis") early in vertebrate evolution duplicated the single ancestral Hox cluster. Extra copies → subfunctionalization (dividing the original gene's roles) and neofunctionalization (evolving entirely new roles) → increased body plan complexity.

Fig 1.5 · Hox in Tetrapods vs. Snakes
Hox gene expression in tetrapods is restricted to limb buds, specifying digit identity. In snakes, regulatory evolution expanded HoxC expression along the full body axis and suppressed the limb-specific Hox program.
Deep Conservation

A mouse Hox gene can partially rescue a Drosophila Hox mutant — and vice versa. The protein-binding homeodomain is so conserved that it functions across 600+ million years of evolution. What has changed is when and where the gene is expressed (regulatory evolution), not the protein itself.

Practice Questions

Bicoid mRNA is injected into the posterior of a normal Drosophila egg. What phenotype do you predict, and why? ▾
A second head/anterior structure forms at the posterior end, producing a double-anterior larva. Bicoid protein is sufficient to activate anterior gap genes (like hunchback) wherever it is present at high concentration. The posterior Nanos gradient is overwhelmed. This experiment proved Bicoid is both necessary AND sufficient for anterior identity — it is the positional signal, not just a permissive factor.
Why do all your cells have the same DNA but different functions? ▾
Differential gene expression. All cells contain the complete genome (~20,000 genes), but each cell type activates only a specific subset. This is controlled by transcription factors, chromatin accessibility (which genes are physically available for transcription), DNA methylation, and histone modifications — all of which can be stably inherited through cell division without changing the DNA sequence. A liver cell and a neuron differ not in their genes but in their gene expression programmes.
Compare Hox gene organisation in Drosophila vs. mammals. What are the evolutionary implications? ▾
Drosophila has 1 Hox cluster with 8 genes (HOM-C). Mammals have 4 clusters (HoxA–D) with 39 genes total, produced by two rounds of whole-genome duplication (2R hypothesis). Extra Hox genes enabled subfunctionalization (dividing the work of an ancestral gene between two paralogs) and neofunctionalization (evolving new roles). This gave vertebrates greater developmental complexity — for example, HoxA13 and HoxD13 can be independently regulated in forelimb vs hindlimb buds, allowing limb morphology to evolve semi-independently. The snake example shows that regulatory changes (not new genes) can produce dramatic body plan differences like complete limb loss.
A mutation eliminates the gap gene Krüppel. What happens to the Drosophila embryo? ▾
Krüppel is expressed in the central region of the embryo and specifies thoracic and abdominal segments T1–A5. Loss of Krüppel causes those segments to be deleted or fused, producing a larva missing much of its middle body. Because gap genes control pair-rule genes, losing Krüppel also disrupts the striped pair-rule expression in the affected region, cascading down to segment polarity and Hox gene misexpression. The phenotype is a "gap" in the segmentation pattern — hence the name.
