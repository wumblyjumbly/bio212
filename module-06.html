⬡ Level 1 · Remember & Identify
Deuterostome Overview

What is a Deuterostome?

◈ Key Distinction

Deuterostomes ("second mouth") are defined by their embryonic development: the blastopore becomes the anus, and the mouth forms secondarily at the opposite end. This contrasts with protostomes, where the blastopore becomes the mouth. Deuterostome cleavage is also radial and indeterminate (cells remain totipotent longer).

Deuterostomes comprise three phyla. Together they include all vertebrates — including humans — as well as sea stars, sea urchins, acorn worms, and lancelets.

⭐
Echinodermata

~7,000 species. Pentaradial symmetry in adults; bilateral larvae. Unique water vascular system. CaCO₃ endoskeleton. Marine only. Sea stars, urchins, sea cucumbers.

🪱
Hemichordata

~130 species. Marine worm-like animals (acorn worms). Possess pharyngeal slits homologous to those in chordates. Important as an outgroup for understanding chordate origins.

🐟
Chordata

~60,000+ species. Defined by four synapomorphies: pharyngeal slits, dorsal hollow nerve cord, notochord, muscular post-anal tail. Includes lancelets, tunicates, and all vertebrates.

Phylum Echinodermata

Echinoderm Synapomorphies

Echinoderms ("spiny skin") share five key derived characters found in no other phylum. All are marine — no freshwater or terrestrial species exist.

Synapomorphy 01

Pentaradial Symmetry

Adults have 5-part radial symmetry. This is derived — echinoderm larvae are bilaterally symmetric, indicating that pentaradial symmetry evolved from a bilateral ancestor. The 5-part symmetry is often modified (e.g., sea urchins have 5 rows of tube feet).

Synapomorphy 02

Water Vascular System (WVS)

A unique hydraulic system of fluid-filled canals. Opens to the outside via the madreporite (sieve plate). Operates tube feet for locomotion, feeding, gas exchange, and sensory functions. Driven by hydrostatic pressure.

Synapomorphy 03

CaCO₃ Endoskeleton

An internal skeleton of calcium carbonate ossicles ("small bones") embedded in the dermis — hence "spiny skin." The skeleton can be rigid (urchins) or flexible (sea stars). In sea cucumbers, ossicles are reduced to microscopic spicules.

Synapomorphy 04

Coelom from Enterocoely

The coelom (body cavity) forms by outpocketing of the gut endoderm — called enterocoely. This is the deuterostome pattern, contrasting with the schizocoely of most protostomes.

Synapomorphy 05

Bilateral Larva

All echinoderms have bilateral, free-swimming larvae that metamorphose into radially symmetric adults. The larva is used as evidence that echinoderms evolved from a bilateral ancestor and as a dispersal stage.

⬡ How the Water Vascular System Works

Sea water enters the madreporite → flows through ring canal → radial canals → tube feet. Contraction of ampullae creates suction. Each tube foot = locomotion + gas exchange + sensory.

Echinoderm Diversity

The Five Classes of Echinoderms

All five classes share the echinoderm synapomorphies above but differ markedly in body form and ecology. Use the tabs below to explore each class.

Class Crinoidea

Feather Stars & Sea Lilies

Antedon, Comanthina, Metacrinus

The most ancient echinoderm class. Sea lilies are sessile — attached via a stalk to the seafloor. Feather stars (comatulids) can detach, crawl, or swim. The mouth faces upward, surrounded by feathery arms with pinnules that suspension-feed on particles. ~600 living species, but ~6,000 extinct — once dominant in shallow Paleozoic seas.

Class Asteroidea

Sea Stars

Asterias, Pisaster ochraceus, Linckia, Acanthaster (crown-of-thorns)

Most have 5 arms that blend into the disc (not sharply distinct). Rows of tube feet run along the oral (ventral) ambulacral groove. Feed by everting the cardiac stomach through the mouth into bivalve prey, digesting externally. Famous for regeneration and as keystone predators — Pisaster removal causes mussel beds to outcompete all other species in rocky intertidal zones.

Class Ophiuroidea

Brittle Stars & Basket Stars

Ophiothrix, Gorgonocephalus (basket star), Amphiura

Key distinction from sea stars: arms are sharply demarcated from the central disc — long, slender, and flexible. "Brittle" = arms snap off easily as autotomy (escape response). Move by lateral arm undulation, not tube feet. No open ambulacral groove. Tube feet reduced; used primarily for feeding. Most species-rich echinoderm class.

Class Echinoidea

Sea Urchins & Sand Dollars

Strongylocentrotus, Arbacia, Lytechinus, Echinus, Dendraster (sand dollar)

Have a rigid, globular test (fused ossicles) — no free arms. Five rows of tube feet pass through pore pairs in the test. Moveable spines protrude from the surface. The unique Aristotle's lantern — 5 CaCO₃ teeth — scrapes algae off rock. Sand dollars are flattened echinoids that burrow in sediment. Strongylocentrotus eggs are the classic model for fertilization and early development.

Class Holothuroidea

Sea Cucumbers

Holothuria, Stichopus, Cucumaria, Apostichopus

The most unusual echinoderms: secondarily bilateral — elongated along the oral-aboral axis and lying on one side. Ossicles are microscopic (body is soft, leathery). Oral tentacles (modified tube feet) collect food. Gas exchange via respiratory trees — branching internal organs irrigated by water pumped through the anus. Defense: evisceration — ejecting sticky organs at predators; tissues regenerate within weeks.

Phylum Chordata

The Four Chordate Synapomorphies

Every member of Phylum Chordata possesses — at some point in its life — all four of the following structures. Some may be lost in adults (as in tunicates) but must be present in the embryo or larva.

◈ Interactive Lancelet Body Plan · Click a feature label to highlight it
1 · Pharyngeal Slits (or Pouches)

Openings in the pharynx wall. Filter-feeding in invertebrate chordates; gill slits in aquatic vertebrates. Present as pouches in all vertebrate embryos.

2 · Dorsal Hollow Nerve Cord

A hollow dorsal tube of nerve tissue. Develops by neurulation from ectoderm. In vertebrates becomes the brain + spinal cord. Contrasts with the solid ventral nerve cord of protostomes.

3 · Notochord

Stiff flexible rod of fluid-filled cells providing body support. Persists in adult cephalochordates. In vertebrates, present in embryos and persists as intervertebral disc cells in adults.

4 · Muscular Post-Anal Tail

Extends posterior to the anus; provides swimming propulsion. Present in all chordate embryos. Lost in tunicates at metamorphosis; the human coccyx is a vestigial form.

Vertebrate Evolution

Vertebrate Phylogeny & Key Lineages

The ~63,000 species of vertebrates are organized into several major clades. The phylogeny below shows the sequence of innovations — each node represents the origin of a new character shared by all descendants.

Vertebrate Innovations

Six Major Evolutionary Innovations

Each innovation is a synapomorphy uniting a clade of vertebrates. Listed in the order they evolved, from oldest to most recent.

🦴
~520 mya · Cambrian · Craniata

Cranium & Vertebrae

The cranium (a bony or cartilaginous case enclosing the brain) and vertebrae (a column of structural supports protecting the spinal cord) define Vertebrata. Hagfishes have a cranium but lost vertebrae. Neural crest cells — a uniquely vertebrate embryonic cell type derived from ectoderm — are responsible for forming the cranium, sensory organs, and many facial structures. Early vertebrate fossils from the Cambrian (~520 mya) show streamlined bodies, gills, cranial cartilage, and paired eyes.

Selective advantage: Protects CNS; enables complex brain development; allows more sophisticated sensory processing and rapid directed movement.

👄
~430 mya · Silurian · Gnathostomata

Jaws & Paired Appendages

Jaws evolved from modified gill arches (anterior pharyngeal arches remodeled into upper and lower jaw bones). The earliest jawed fishes are the placoderms — armored fishes with bony head shields that appear in the fossil record ~430 mya. Paired fins also appear with jaws. Many ray-finned fishes have a second set of jaws called pharyngeal jaws (modified gill arches in the throat) for food processing. Teeth appeared soon after jaws. More than 99% of living vertebrate species are gnathostomes.

Selective advantage: Jaws enable biting and chewing — vertebrates no longer limited to filter/deposit feeding. Access to new food types (herbivory, predation). Paired fins improve maneuverability in water.

🦷
Late Silurian · Osteichthyes

Bony Endoskeleton & Lungs

In early osteichthyans (bony fishes), the cartilaginous endoskeleton was stiffened by calcium phosphate bone deposition — the bony endoskeleton. Unlike earlier dermal bone (derived from neural crest cells / ectoderm), the internal bony endoskeleton was derived from mesoderm and supported swimming. Simultaneously, lungs appeared as outpockets of the esophagus — originally as an auxiliary oxygen source for the heart in oxygen-poor shallow water. In ray-finned fishes, lungs were co-opted as a swim bladder for neutral buoyancy. Lungs were lost in cartilaginous fishes.

Selective advantage: Bone is stronger, enabling larger body sizes. Lungs solve oxygen limitation in stagnant water; swim bladder enables energy-efficient buoyancy.

🦵
~365 mya · Devonian · Tetrapoda

Tetrapod Limbs

The first vertebrates with limbs capable of supporting movement on land appear in the fossil record ~365 mya (late Devonian). Tetrapod limbs evolved from the lobed fins of sarcopterygians (lobe-finned fishes, closest living relatives: lungfishes). Key fossil sequence: Eusthenopteron (lobe fin, ~385 mya) → Tiktaalik (~375 mya, has wrist-like fin with humerus/radius/ulna) → Ichthyostega (~365 mya, first true tetrapod). Each fin/limb has: one proximal bone → two bones → distal elements — a pattern conserved across all tetrapods. Same Hox genes pattern fish fins and mammal limbs. Three lineages evolved from a single water-to-land transition: amphibians, mammals, reptiles.

Selective advantage: Access to terrestrial habitats; escape from aquatic predators; access to new food sources; higher oxygen availability on land.

🥚
~340 mya · Carboniferous · Amniota

Amniotic Egg

The amniotic egg is a synapomorphy of Amniota (all tetrapods except amphibians). It contains four extraembryonic membranes:
• Amnion — surrounds embryo in fluid; protects from desiccation and mechanical shock
• Chorion — outer membrane; surrounds everything; gas exchange
• Yolk sac — encloses nutrients
• Allantois — stores nitrogenous wastes; facilitates gas exchange
The membranes provide mechanical support outside water and increase surface area for gas/nutrient diffusion. The shell (leathery in lizards/snakes, calcified in birds) reduces drying. In viviparous reptiles and mammals, the egg is retained internally — the four membranes persist (allantois + chorion form the placenta in mammals).

Selective advantage: Reproduction fully independent of standing water. Larger eggs → larger, more independent hatchlings. Colonization of dry terrestrial environments.

🐦
Independent origins · Mammalia + Aves

Endothermy, Fur & Feathers

Endothermy (self-heating; "warm-bloodedness") evolved independently in mammals and birds — a striking example of convergent evolution. Both lineages regulate body temperature by generating heat metabolically. Ectothermic reptiles rely on behavioral thermoregulation. In mammals, fur (layers of keratin hair) insulates the body; mammary glands produce milk (lactation). In birds (Aves), feathers evolved in stages within dinosaurs (initially for insulation/display) before being co-opted for flight. Both fur and feathers are derived from keratin — homologous molecular pathways to reptile scales. Several additional bird adaptations enable flight: hollow bones, keeled sternum, large breast muscles, and endothermy sustaining high activity year-round.

Selective advantage: Year-round activity; survival at low temperatures; nocturnal activity (mammals); sustained powered flight (birds). Endothermy is energetically expensive but allows high, consistent metabolic output.

Vertebrate Diversity

Major Vertebrate Lineages

The Cyclostomata (round-mouthed): hagfishes and lampreys are jawless vertebrates, united by microRNA evidence. Hagfishes (~78 sp.) are marine scavengers/predators — they deposit-feed on carcasses, have a cranium but no vertebrae (vertebrae were secondarily lost), use the notochord for support, and secrete copious defensive slime. Lampreys (~46 sp.) are mostly ectoparasites with a sucker-like mouth lined with teeth to rasp holes in fish hosts and suck blood. Lamprey larvae are filter-feeding burrowers that resemble lancelets — they metamorphose into adults after years.

~1,200 species (sharks, rays, skates, chimaeras). Jawed gnathostomes with a cartilaginous skeleton reinforced by calcium salts (not true bone). Dermal scales are small tooth-like denticles (homologous to teeth). Lost lungs. Sharks swim by undulating body; rays flap wing-like pectoral fins. ~32,200 species — the most species-rich vertebrate group. Fins are supported by bony rays in a fan pattern; body covered in interlocking scales. Many have a swim bladder (derived from lungs via co-option) for neutral buoyancy. Remarkable jaw diversity: many can extend protrusible jaws to suction-feed; several lineages have pharyngeal jaws for food processing. Teleosts (a subgroup) underwent a massive adaptive radiation after the K-Pg extinction ~66 mya. About half of all vertebrate species.

Coelacanths (2 species; Latimeria): thought extinct since Cretaceous; rediscovered alive in 1938. Fleshy lobed fins supported by bones in the 1-2-many pattern (homologous to tetrapod limbs). Lungfishes (6 species; freshwater): have true lungs and use them to breathe air when aquatic oxygen drops. Some burrow in mud during drought (aestivation). Walk on lobed fins. Lungfishes are the closest living relatives of tetrapods. The fin skeleton pattern in lungfishes and Tiktaalik provided key evidence for the limbs-from-fins hypothesis.

First tetrapods to live on land (~365 mya). Name "both-sides-living" reflects dual life: most adults feed on land but lay eggs in water. Three orders: Anura (frogs/toads, 7,400 sp. — tailless adults, aquatic tadpoles metamorphose), Urodela (salamanders, 675 sp. — tails retained, can regenerate limbs), Caecilians (200 sp. — limbless, burrowing). Gas exchange occurs in part or entirely through moist, mucus-covered skin. Eggs have no shell and must remain moist — reproduction still tied to water.

Defined by: mammary glands (milk production / lactation), fur, endothermy, one bone in lower jaw, three middle ear bones. Three major lineages:

  • Monotremata (5 sp. — platypus, echidnas): egg-laying; secrete milk from skin glands, no nipples; lower metabolic rate; Australia & New Guinea only.
  • Marsupialia (~350 sp. — kangaroos, koalas, opossums): viviparous with simple placenta; young born very underdeveloped and complete development in pouch; convergent evolution with placental mammals in feeding strategies.
  • Eutheria / Placentalia (~5,100 sp.): extended gestation with elaborate placenta (allantois + chorion form placenta); 29 orders; by far the most diverse — rodents (2,255 sp.), bats (1,150 sp.), primates, carnivorans, etc.

Reptiles are the second amniote clade (sister to mammals). Defined by: keratin scales or feathers, amniotic eggs, well-developed lungs. Four major lineages:

  • Lepidosauria (lizards & snakes, 9,800 sp.): ectotherms; snakes lost limbs secondarily; parthenogenesis in some.
  • Testudinia (turtles, 340 sp.): shell is fused bony plates joined to vertebrae and ribs; all oviparous; can live >150 years.
  • Crocodilia (25 sp.): closest living relatives of birds; extensive parental care (unique among non-avian reptiles); top aquatic predators.
  • Aves / Birds (10,000 sp.): phylogenetically within dinosaurs — birds ARE dinosaurs. Feathers (insulation → display → flight), hollow bones, keeled sternum, endothermy, complex parental care. Flight evolved independently in birds, pterosaurs, and bats.

◈ Correct Order & Why
  1. Bone tissue — dermal bone (neural crest origin) appeared first in placoderms; used as external armor (head shields) before internal skeleton was bony.
  2. Bony endoskeleton — internal bone (mesodermal origin) appeared later in osteichthyans (bony fishes); provided structural support for swimming.

  5. Amniotic egg — Carboniferous (~340 mya), ~20 million years after tetrapods; freed reproduction from water.
Selective Pressures Analysis

What Problem Did Each Innovation Solve?

For each innovation, consider: what was the environmental challenge? and what could the organism now do that its ancestor could not?

Innovation

Jaws

Environmental challenge: Competition for food; large prey items available but inaccessible. Jawless vertebrates limited to filter/deposit feeding or parasitism.

New capability: Bite and capture prey; process plant material; diversify into herbivory, predation, omnivory. Triggered radiation of gnathostomes that dominates today.

Innovation

Lungs

Environmental challenge: Shallow, warm, oxygen-poor water (Devonian swamps). Gills alone insufficient for oxygen delivery to the heart in hypoxic conditions.

New capability: Supplement gill respiration with air-breathing. Later co-opted as swim bladder (buoyancy) in ray-finned fish. Set stage for air-breathing tetrapods.

Innovation

Tetrapod Limbs

Environmental challenge: Untapped terrestrial habitats; aquatic predation pressure; fluctuating water levels in Devonian floodplains.

New capability: Locomotion on land. Access to terrestrial food sources; escape from aquatic predators; dispersal across land. Limbs later diversified into running, flying, burrowing, swimming forms.

Innovation

Amniotic Egg

Environmental challenge: Desiccation on land; amphibian eggs dry out without water. Reproductive constraint ties amphibians to aquatic environments.

New capability: Lay eggs in dry terrestrial environments. Protected embryo from desiccation. Allows colonization of deserts, mountains, tropical canopies — anywhere.

Innovation

Endothermy

Environmental challenge: Temperature fluctuations; cold nights and winters prevent ectotherms from activity. Competition for nocturnal/cold-weather niches.

New capability: Maintain high, consistent body temperature. Year-round activity; high aerobic capacity; sustained exertion. Trade-off: requires ~10× more food than ectotherms of same size.

Innovation

Placenta (Eutherian)

Environmental challenge: Oviparous reproduction exposes eggs to predation and environmental extremes. Marsupial young are highly vulnerable at birth.

New capability: Extended in utero development; highly developed young at birth; mother not tied to a nest. Allows exploitation of unstable environments. Trade-off: energetically costly; risky for mother.

Level 3 · Synthesis

Convergent Evolution in Vertebrates

Evolution is not a linear progression. The same trait has evolved independently multiple times — this is convergent evolution, indicating that certain environments strongly favor particular solutions.

Three times independently:

  • Pterosaurs (extinct reptiles, ~230–66 mya) — membranous wings supported by elongated 4th finger; the largest reaching 10 m wingspan.
  • Birds (Aves) (~125 mya origin with feathered dinosaurs) — wings are modified forelimbs with feathers; hollow bones, keeled sternum, endothermy.
  • Bats (Chiroptera) — membranous wings supported by elongated fingers; the only flying mammals; 2nd most species-rich mammalian order (~1,150 sp.).

All three convergently evolved powered, flapping flight as a solution to accessing aerial food resources and three-dimensional habitats.

Viviparity evolved at least 100 times independently across vertebrates — one of the most convergent traits in evolution:

  • Cartilaginous fishes (some sharks): internal fertilization and viviparity; a placenta evolved independently in certain shark lineages.
  • Ray-finned fishes: some lineages independently evolved viviparity.
  • Reptiles: viviparity has evolved >100 times in lizards and snakes alone.
  • Mammals: marsupials and eutherians both viviparous but placenta structure differs dramatically.

Selective advantage: offspring protected from predation and temperature fluctuations; mother controls embryo environment.

Limb loss is adaptive in burrowing/swimming lifestyles:

  • Snakes: descended from lizards with four limbs; limbs lost for burrowing/constricting lifestyle. Vestigial pelvic bones remain in some (boas, pythons).
  • Caecilians: limbless amphibians; convergently evolved for burrowing life.
  • Whales & dolphins: tetrapods that returned to the sea; hindlimbs lost; forelimbs modified into flippers. Vestigial pelvis and hindlimb bones remain internally.
  • Manatees & dugongs: similar secondary return to water, limb modification.

Lesson: evolution is not unidirectional from simple to complex. Loss is adaptive.

Yes — two independent origins: Mammals and birds are the only living fully endothermic vertebrates, but they evolved endothermy from different ectothermic ancestors.

  • Mammals: evolved endothermy from the synapsid (mammal-like reptile) lineage. Insulated by fur (keratin hair).
  • Birds: evolved endothermy within the theropod dinosaur lineage. Insulated by feathers. Evidence from dinosaur bone growth rings suggests some dinosaurs were mesotherms (intermediate).

Same selective pressure (thermal regulation, high activity level) → same solution (internal heat generation) → two independent origins.
