⬡ Level 1 · Remember & Identify
Species Concepts

Three Species Concepts

There is no single universally accepted definition of a species. Three major concepts are each useful in different contexts — but each has limitations.

BSC · Biological Species Concept

Biological Species Concept

A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring, and are reproductively isolated from other groups.

Proposed by Ernst Mayr (1942)

⚠ Cannot apply to asexual organisms, fossils, or populations that rarely encounter each other. Ring species violate neat categories.
MSC · Morphological Species Concept

Morphological Species Concept

A species is defined by its physical characteristics (morphology). Organisms that look similar enough belong to the same species.

Oldest concept; used in paleontology and taxonomy

⚠ Subjective — how different is different enough? Cryptic species (look identical but can't interbreed) and polymorphic species (same species, different morphs) cause problems.
PSC · Phylogenetic Species Concept

Phylogenetic Species Concept

A species is the smallest group of organisms that shares a unique common ancestor — identifiable by one or more derived character states (synapomorphies).

Phylogenetically rigorous; works for all organisms

⚠ Tends to "split" species into many smaller units, dramatically increasing species count. Makes conservation listings complex.
Tree of Life

Anatomy of a Phylogenetic Tree

Annotated cladogram — hover over labels to understand each part

Node
A branching point on a phylogenetic tree representing a hypothetical common ancestor of the lineages that diverge from it.
Branch
A line connecting nodes and tips. Represents an evolutionary lineage. Branch length can represent time or amount of change.
Tip (Leaf)
Terminal taxa at the end of branches — the organisms being classified (extant species, extinct species, or sequences).
Character
Any heritable feature that can be compared across taxa — morphological (four limbs), molecular (gene sequence), or behavioural.
Ancestral Trait
A character state inherited from a remote ancestor — shared broadly across a group (plesiomorphy). Example: vertebral column in vertebrates.
Derived Trait
A character state that evolved in a particular lineage and differs from the ancestral state (apomorphy). Shared derived traits (synapomorphies) define monophyletic groups.
Similarity Types

Homology vs. Homoplasy

Homology

Similarity due to shared ancestry. Homologous structures share a common origin regardless of current function.

Example: Human arm, bat wing, whale flipper — all share the same bone arrangement (humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, phalanges) inherited from a tetrapod ancestor.

Homoplasy

Similarity due to convergent evolution, not shared ancestry. Independent origins of similar structures (or reversals).

Example: Wings of birds and butterflies — superficially similar, but evolved completely independently. Also: streamlined body shape in sharks (fish) and dolphins (mammals).

Key distinction: Homology is evidence for common descent — it is used to build phylogenetic trees. Homoplasy is misleading in phylogenetics because it produces the same trait through different evolutionary paths, causing trees to be incorrect if not detected.
⬡ Level 2 · Understand & Explain
Speciation Mechanics

Divergence vs. Speciation Prevention

Processes that CAUSE Divergence (→ Speciation)

  • Natural selection — different environments favour different phenotypes in isolated populations
  • Genetic drift — random fixation of different alleles in isolated populations
  • Mutation — new alleles arise independently in each population
  • Geographic isolation (allopatry) — prevents gene exchange

Process that PREVENTS Speciation

  • Gene flow — continual mixing of alleles between populations homogenises gene pools, preventing divergence and reproductive isolation
High gene flow = populations stay genetically similar = speciation unlikely

Reproductive Barriers

Speciation requires reproductive isolation — barriers that prevent gene flow between populations.

Pre-Zygotic Barriers

Prevent fertilization

  • Habitat isolation
  • Temporal isolation (different mating seasons)
  • Behavioural isolation (different courtship)
  • Mechanical isolation (incompatible structures)
  • Gametic isolation (sperm can't fertilise egg)
Post-Zygotic Barriers

Prevent gene flow after fertilization

  • Hybrid inviability (hybrid dies early)
  • Hybrid sterility (e.g., mule — horse × donkey)
  • Hybrid breakdown (F₂ generation infertile)
Reading Trees

Monophyletic, Paraphyletic & Polyphyletic Groups

✓ Valid Clade

Monophyletic (Clade)

Includes a common ancestor and ALL of its descendants. This is the only grouping that reflects true evolutionary history.

Example: Mammals — all descend from a single ancestral amniote lineage, including all living mammal species.

✗ Artificial

Paraphyletic

Includes a common ancestor but excludes some of its descendants. Common in older, non-phylogenetic classifications.

Example: "Reptiles" (traditional) — excludes birds, even though birds descended from theropod dinosaurs (reptile lineage).

✗ Artificial

Polyphyletic

Includes organisms from multiple unrelated lineages grouped together by convergent traits rather than shared ancestry.

Example: "Warm-blooded animals" — groups birds and mammals together despite having separate evolutionary origins of endothermy.

Four+ Biases in the Fossil Record

The fossil record is an incomplete and biased sample of past life. Understanding its limitations is essential to interpreting it correctly.

Hard Parts Bias

Hard structures (bone, shell, teeth) fossilise far more readily than soft tissues (muscle, organs, skin). Soft-bodied organisms (worms, jellyfish, slugs) are rarely preserved — the fossil record drastically under-represents them.

Habitat Bias

Fossilisation requires burial in sediment. Aquatic and lowland environments are far better represented than upland, forest, or arid environments where sediment rarely accumulates.

Abundance Bias

Rare organisms are unlikely to be discovered even if preserved. Common, widespread organisms with large populations are much more likely to appear in the fossil record, skewing our view toward dominant taxa.

Temporal Bias

Older rocks are rarer (buried deeper, lost to tectonic processes) and have had more time for fossils to be destroyed. Ancient life is dramatically under-sampled compared to more recent organisms.

Geographic Bias

Paleontological research is concentrated in accessible, politically stable, well-funded regions. Large areas of the tropics, deep ocean floors, and remote regions are poorly explored — many organisms await discovery.

Preservation Checklist

Check which conditions apply to an organism — more checks = more likely to fossilize:

Mineralized structures (calcium carbonate, phosphate, silica) resist decomposition and can be replaced by minerals over time. Organisms with hard parts are 10–100× more likely to fossilize than soft-bodied relatives.
Sediment continuously accumulates in water bodies, rapidly burying organisms before decomposition. ~80% of all known fossil species are marine, even though marine environments cover only ~70% of Earth's surface.
Fossilization is statistically rare — perhaps 1 in 10,000 individuals per generation. Large populations provide vastly more opportunities. Rare specialist species with <100 individuals have a near-zero chance of leaving any fossil.
Scavengers, bacteria, and chemical weathering destroy remains within weeks to years if exposed. Rapid burial (landslide, flood, volcanic ash, turbidite) can preserve even soft tissues — as seen in the Burgess Shale and Pompei-type deposits.
Tectonic activity, volcanic heat, and metamorphism destroy fossils. Ancient cratons (stable continental cores) preserve the oldest fossil-bearing rocks. Subduction zones recycle oceanic crust — destroying any marine fossils within.
Fossil-bearing rock becomes scarcer and more disturbed with age. Cenozoic fossils (last 66 Mya) are ~10× more abundant per taxonomic group than Mesozoic ones; Precambrian fossil-bearing rock is exceedingly rare.

Hox Gene Cluster Duplication & Body Plan Diversification

One of the most striking discoveries of evolutionary developmental biology is that major increases in animal complexity correlate with whole-genome duplications of the Hox gene clusters. More Hox clusters = more developmental toolkit = more complex, diverse body plans.

The 2R Hypothesis (Ohno, 1970): The vertebrate ancestor underwent two rounds of whole-genome duplication. This quadrupled the number of Hox clusters (and many other regulatory genes), providing the raw genetic material for major innovations: complex paired eyes, elaborate limbs, sophisticated brains, and the neural crest.

Paralogous genes (same number, different cluster) arose from whole-genome duplications. Note that not all 13 positions are occupied in every cluster — some duplicated genes were lost over evolutionary time.

Scenario Practice

Apply Species Concepts to Real Examples

Read each scenario and determine which species concept is most applicable, and how it resolves the question.

Scenario 01

Two populations of frogs live on opposite sides of a mountain range. They have never been observed in contact. Their calls differ slightly, and their coloration differs, but molecular data suggests they are nearly identical genetically. A researcher wants to determine whether they are one species or two. Which species concept is most appropriate — and what evidence would be most useful?

BSC is most informative but cannot be directly applied without observing interbreeding. The BSC requires testing: bring populations together and see if they interbreed and produce fertile offspring. The MSC would classify them differently based on colour/call. The PSC might keep them as one species if they share no unique derived characters. This scenario highlights why no single concept is perfect — and why a researcher might use all three and triangulate.
Scenario 02

A paleontologist discovers a series of fossils with subtly different shell shapes across 10 million years of rock layers. She wants to identify which fossils represent distinct species and which are within-species variation. Which species concept must she use, and why?

The Morphological Species Concept (MSC) — she has no choice. Fossils cannot be tested for interbreeding (BSC fails). Without molecular phylogenetic data, the PSC is also difficult to apply. She must use morphological criteria (shell shape, size, ornamentation) to delineate species boundaries. This is the standard approach in paleontology, but it is acknowledged as subjective.
Scenario 03

Bacteria reproduce asexually. A microbiologist proposes that two strains of bacteria that cause slightly different diseases are separate species. The BSC cannot help her. Which concept should she use, and what character would define the new species?

The Phylogenetic Species Concept (PSC) is most applicable. Since BSC requires sexual reproduction (impossible here) and MSC is subjective, the PSC offers the most rigorous framework: identify a unique derived character state (e.g., a specific mutation in a virulence gene, a unique 16S rRNA sequence motif) that defines a monophyletic lineage. This is why bacterial taxonomy increasingly uses genomic data and the PSC.
Scenario 04

Ensatina salamanders form a "ring species" around California's Central Valley. Adjacent populations interbreed freely, but the two end populations (where the ring closes in southern California) do not interbreed when they meet. Are they one species or two?

This violates the BSC's clean categories. Under BSC: the end populations do not interbreed → two species. But every adjacent pair along the ring does interbreed → one species. There is no non-arbitrary answer under BSC. The MSC might classify them as slightly different morphospecies. The PSC would identify each distinct lineage as a separate species. Ring species are a classic demonstration of why no single species concept perfectly describes the continuum of divergence in nature — evolution does not respect our definitions.
Practice Questions

Test Your Understanding

Q1: A phylogenetic tree shows taxa A, B, C, D, and E. A group includes A, B, and their most recent common ancestor, but excludes C, D, and E (which also descended from that ancestor). What type of group is this?
Paraphyletic. The group includes the common ancestor but excludes some of its descendants (C, D, E). A monophyletic group would include the ancestor AND all descendants. Example: "Reptilia" traditionally excludes birds despite birds being descended from reptiles.
Q2: Why might a fossil of a soft-bodied organism from 500 mya be essentially impossible to find, even if those organisms were extremely abundant in their time?
Fossilisation requires preservation of hard parts (bias #1), rapid burial (bias #2), and survival through hundreds of millions of years of geological processes including subduction, metamorphism, and erosion (temporal bias). Soft-bodied organisms decompose rapidly with no hard parts to preserve. Even if buried, soft tissue leaves no mineral-replaced fossil. The Burgess Shale (Canada) is exceptional precisely because unusual low-oxygen, fine-sediment conditions preserved soft tissues.
Q3: A student argues: "Bat wings and bird wings are homologous — both are wings used for flight, so they must have the same evolutionary origin." Correct this argument.
This is incorrect. Bat wings and bird wings are homoplastic (convergent), not homologous. Homology is defined by shared ancestry, not function. Bat wings evolved from the modified forelimb of a mammalian ancestor (membrane stretches between elongated finger bones). Bird wings evolved from the forelimb of a theropod dinosaur ancestor (feathers on a fused hand). The forelimbs themselves are homologous (shared tetrapod ancestor), but the flight wing structure evolved independently in each lineage.
Q4: The Cambrian Explosion (~540 mya) saw an apparent rapid diversification of most animal phyla. How does Hox gene duplication provide a mechanistic explanation for this event?
By ~540 mya, the vertebrate ancestor had undergone whole-genome duplications expanding its Hox cluster number (the 2R hypothesis suggests 4 clusters were present in jawed vertebrates). More Hox genes mean more positional identity codes available along the body axis — more ways to specify different body segments differently. This expanded developmental toolkit allowed the evolution of novel, complex body plans (distinct head regions, segmented appendages, specialised anterior vs. posterior structures) that were impossible with a single Hox cluster. The duplication of regulatory genes generally (not just Hox) provided raw material for diversification via subfunctionalisation and neofunctionalisation.
